# FGF18 in Knee Osteoarthritis

## Gene Overview

Fibroblast Growth Factor 18 (FGF18) is a secreted signaling protein in the FGF family, encoded by the *FGF18* gene on human chromosome 5q35.1. Within the joint, FGF18 signals predominantly through FGF receptor 3 (FGFR3) on articular chondrocytes. Unlike the catabolic FGF2 (which predominantly signals through FGFR1), FGF18 acts as an anabolic factor for cartilage — stimulating chondrocyte proliferation and matrix synthesis.

FGF18 is expressed in perichondrium, synovium, and articular cartilage at physiologic levels. Its expression is developmentally regulated and contributes to endochondral ossification and joint morphogenesis.

## Role in Osteoarthritis

The therapeutic rationale for FGF18 in osteoarthritis is anabolic: rather than inhibiting cartilage degradation (the MMP13/ADAMTS5 approach) or suppressing inflammation (the IL-6 approach), FGF18 aims to **actively stimulate cartilage regeneration** in the OA joint.

Mechanistically, FGF18 signaling through FGFR3 on articular chondrocytes:
- Stimulates proliferation of superficial and middle-zone chondrocytes
- Increases type II collagen and aggrecan synthesis
- Suppresses terminal hypertrophic differentiation (which would otherwise lead to ossification)
- Promotes a stable articular chondrocyte phenotype

The anabolic mechanism is distinct from the catabolic-inhibition mechanisms of most OA drug candidates. This makes FGF18 a "structure-modifying OA drug" (SMOAD) candidate that could in principle reverse — not just halt — cartilage loss.

## Key Evidence

- **Preclinical efficacy:** Intra-articular FGF18 injection in rat and rabbit OA models produces dose-dependent increases in cartilage thickness and improved histological OA scores (Shimoaka et al., 2002).
- **FGFR3 is the relevant receptor:** FGFR3 knockout mice show accelerated OA-like cartilage degeneration, whereas FGFR1-knockout mice are partially protected — confirming the receptor-selectivity of FGF18's anabolic action.
- **Sprifermin clinical development (FORWARD trial):** Sprifermin (recombinant human FGF18) was developed by Merck KGaA and later Novartis. The FORWARD Phase II study (NCT01919164) in ~550 patients with symptomatic knee OA showed statistically significant increases in total femorotibial cartilage thickness at 2 years, with dose-dependent responses (Hochberg et al., 2019; JAMA; PMID 31621831).
- **Dose-response and durability:** The 100 µg dose every 6 or 12 months produced the largest cartilage thickness gains. Structural benefits were maintained through the 5-year FORWARD follow-up.

## Clinical Relevance

Despite structural benefit signals, FGF18/sprifermin faces specific translational challenges:

1. **Symptom-structure gap:** The FORWARD trial demonstrated structural improvement (cartilage thickness by quantitative MRI) but did not show parallel symptomatic benefit in the overall population. This is a well-known challenge for SMOADs: the regulatory path (FDA, EMA) traditionally requires both structural AND symptomatic endpoints. Subgroup analyses suggested benefit in patients with more severe baseline disease.

2. **Delivery and dosing:** Intra-articular injection every 6-12 months is feasible but places a burden on patients compared with oral or sustained-release therapies. Long-acting formulations are an active area of development.

3. **Selectivity and receptor biology:** Off-target FGFR1 or FGFR2 activation could theoretically cause adverse effects (hyperphosphatemia, cartilage calcification). Sprifermin showed an acceptable safety profile in FORWARD, but receptor-selective next-generation analogues are an R&D priority.

4. **Combination therapy potential:** FGF18's anabolic mechanism is complementary to catabolic inhibitors (MMP13, ADAMTS5) and anti-inflammatories (IL-6). Combination regimens are a plausible late-stage strategy.

5. **Patient stratification:** Responders in FORWARD appeared to have more severe baseline disease. Enrichment strategies using MRI cartilage morphometry, serum biomarkers (CTX-II, C2C), or OA phenotype classification could improve trial success probability.

## Summary

FGF18 / sprifermin is the most clinically advanced anabolic cartilage-regenerative therapy for OA, with robust preclinical support, demonstrated Phase II structural benefit, and durable long-term data. The remaining challenges are demonstrating symptomatic benefit, optimizing delivery, and identifying the patient subpopulation most likely to benefit — not questions of target validity.
